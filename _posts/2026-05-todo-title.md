University tests your work ethic, your cooking skills, your academic integrity, and more - but most importantly, your perseverance. 

Throughout most of school, we are taught that succcess means high marks, constant engagement in lessons, extracurricular activites, and passing all your exams. At univeristy, this mask begins to slip - where you were once supported by your parents, if you were lucky enough to have a stable home environment, has now been replaced by a harsh reality of individuality. Loud flatmates keeping you up by shouting at videogames, loneliness-inducing nights where you stare at the grey and still darkening sky out of the window, and deciding whether you can eat the same leftover pasta for the fifth day in a row. These are all problems I have faced - and all while behind on lectures and my brain full with thoughts about my assignments due at the end of the week. Along with all of that, you have to juggle friendships, birthdays, parties, going out, keeping up the pretense that everybody maintains that you can balance thirty weekly contact hours and a solid social life, and any drama or fallings-out you are unfortunate enough to have found yourself in this week. There's a lot of that at university, more than people think. 

I always thought people would grow up - from primary school to secondary school, from secondary school to college, and from college to sixth form - and while it nominally happens, for some people, it really doesn't seem to sink in that they're twenty years old. Way too many university students have the social abilities of a sixteen year old, and the hormones to match - avoiding drama at university is the best way to keep your grades high and your mental health intact - people don't grow up. It makes me honestly worried for the world of work - am I really going to have to deal with Carl, who was accidentally excluded from a birthday party groupchat, throwing a hissy fit? At his big age? Apparently, yes. 

University proves whether you are ready to go out into the big wide world. It's a stepping stone - it's not quite yet reality; your landlords are often muted and abstracted away, faceless companies who manage student housing - you are still being taught things, and depending on your course and university, you still have professors to talk to and learn from. For all intents and purposes, you're still a student in education - you are simply allowed certain privileges, like being allowed to cook for yourself, to manage your own finances, your own relationships, and your own schedule. For the first time in your life, you can sleep as late as you want! No bedtime! But you will soon realise, you also have the freedom to sleep as early as you want, and this soon becomes more attractive. Even better, infinite snacks! You can eat a chocolate cake every day! But as your wallet and digestive system will soon tell you, you can just as easily eat healthier than you may have back home, and live all the better for it. All this, not to even mention juggling sleeping and eating around the much more important thing - studying. At university, human bodily needs like sleep and hunger are secondary to your assignments. 

Some people find out they can't hack it. That's alright - they leave, they drop out, they switch university, they switch course. There's no shame in that. Some people just quietly quit - you won't see them at lectures much anymore, they'll hand in substandard work; but they'll survive, they'll keep going. THat's also valid - but a drudgingly painful reality for them is that they're wasting the best days of their lives. Or so we keep getting told. 

I think that's largely a myth. You make the best days of your life by making them the best days of your life - not by simply sitting back and waiting for them to wash over you. I know several people who would confidently tell you that their forties were the best years of their life, if they had a terrible time at university, but bounced back and had a mid-life confidence boost. I think that's more common than you think.
